Gardens for
Everyone

A design project on making Glasgow Botanic Gardens accessible for blind and deaf visitors.

See Our Proposed Solution View Our Work

The Problem Statement

The Challenge: Volunteer guides at Glasgow Botanic Gardens want to enhance their tours to be more meaningful and enjoyable for deaf and/or blind visitors. However, existing tour formats rely heavily on visual and auditory elements, creating significant barriers for visitors with sensory impairments.

Key Barriers Identified:

For Blind Visitors: Lack of tactile elements, audio descriptions, and spatial orientation support
For Deaf Visitors: No sign language interpretation, visual information gaps, limited text-based content
Guide Challenges: Limited training in accessibility practices and lack of inclusive tour materials
Resource Constraints: Public venue with limited budget for accessibility improvements

How might we...

Design an accessible, cost-effective digital solution that empowers volunteer guides to deliver meaningful, inclusive botanical garden experiences for blind and deaf visitors, while enhancing rather than replacing human interaction?

Double Diamond Design Process

1

Discover

We conducted an informal, face-to-face interview with our partner and researched existing solutions in other public attractions.

Methods: Stakeholder interviews, preexisting solutions analysis
2

Define

We synthesised our research into key insights and formulated our "How Might We" statement to focus our efforts.

Methods: Insight synthesis, problem reframing, constraint mapping
3

We brainstormed diverse ideas and created low-fidelity prototypes (sketches) to explore potential solutions.

Methods: Lo-fi prototyping, concept validation
4

Deliver

This final stage involves refining our proposed solution based on feedback and developing a clear implementation plan.

Methods: Hi-fi prototyping

Process Deep Dive

Discovery Challenges & Breakthroughs

Initial assumption: "We need to design one accessible tour." Reality from stakeholder interviews: Different disabilities require fundamentally different approaches.

Breakthrough moment: When volunteers explained how deaf and blind visitors need opposite information (visual detail vs. spatial audio), we realized one-size-fits-all was impossible.

Define Phase Iterations

Our "How Might We" statement evolved three times: from "create accessible tours" to "enhance existing tours" to "empower volunteers to deliver inclusive experiences."

Key shift: Recognizing volunteers as users, not just stakeholders, completely changed our solution approach.

Development Pivot Points

Original concept: Physical audio devices. Pivot 1: Mobile app. Pivot 2: Web-based solution. Each iteration was driven by constraint recognition (cost, maintenance, app store friction).

Learning: Constraints are not limitations, they are design criteria that lead to more sustainable solutions.

Delivery & Future Validation

Our prototypes demonstrate concept viability but represent beginning, not end, of design process. Implementation would require continuous user feedback and iterative refinement.

Next steps: Partner with disability organizations for user co-design sessions and technical validation through working prototypes.

Our Proposed Solution

Based on our process and research, here is our accessibility solution for Glasgow Botanic Gardens.

Visual Tour Mode (For Deaf Visitors)

A mobile-responsive web application featuring QR code scanning at key garden locations, providing access to British Sign Language (BSL) video interpretations, visual descriptions, high-resolution botanical photography, and text-based information tailored for deaf and hard-of-hearing visitors.

Audio Tour Mode (For Blind Visitors)

An accessible audio-first interface with location-based navigation menus, professional narration with rich botanical descriptions, ambient garden sounds, and screen reader-optimized controls. Users can select specific garden areas and plants for detailed auditory experiences.

Why This Solution Works

Our dual-mode approach directly addresses the different needs we discovered through our research. It is low-cost (leveraging visitors' smartphones), sustainable (easy for volunteers to manage), and truly inclusive (tailored experiences rather than one-size-fits-all).

From Process to Reflection

Our journey through research, design, and development has given us valuable insights about inclusive design, stakeholder collaboration, and the iterative nature of solving complex problems. Here are our honest reflections on what we learned, what we are proud of, and what we would do differently.

Reflections

Reflecting on our design journey, we share our proudest achievements, key learnings, and insights from working with our partners.

Reflection on Partner Interactions

Our collaboration with the Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens proved to be one of the most valuable and eye-opening aspects of this project.

Initial Interview Insights

Our face-to-face interview with the volunteer guides revealed constraints we had not initially considered. The volunteers emphasized the need for low-cost, sustainable solutions that would not overwhelm their existing resources or require extensive technical training.

Key learning: Real-world constraints often drive the most innovative solutions. The budget and maintenance limitations pushed us toward smartphone-based solutions rather than expensive custom hardware.

Ongoing Communication

Throughout the design process, we maintained regular contact with our partners to validate our assumptions and gather feedback on our evolving prototypes. Their input helped us refine our understanding of what would actually work in practice.

Key learning: Stakeholder engagement should not be a one-time event. Continuous dialogue ensures solutions remain grounded in real needs rather than theoretical assumptions.

What We Learned About Partnership

Working with the Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens taught us that successful design partnerships require more than just gathering requirements. They demand empathy, active listening, and a willingness to see problems through the lens of those who will implement and maintain the solutions.

The volunteers' passion for making the gardens accessible to everyone was infectious and helped us stay motivated when tackling complex accessibility challenges. Their practical wisdom about visitor behavior and garden operations grounded our design decisions in reality.

Most importantly: We learned that the best design solutions emerge from genuine collaboration, where both designers and stakeholders contribute unique expertise to solve shared challenges.

Reflecting on Our Work

What We Are Proud Of

Accessibility-First Approach

We are particularly proud of how we approached accessibility not as an afterthought, but as the core design principle. Rather than creating one solution and adapting it, we designed two distinct but complementary experiences tailored to the specific needs of deaf and blind visitors.

Realistic Constraint Handling

Our solution respects the real-world constraints of a volunteer-run organization. By leveraging visitors' smartphones and creating maintainable digital content, we have designed something that could actually be implemented and sustained.

The evolution from lo-fi to hi-fi prototypes represents genuine learning. Our initial sketches captured spatial concepts well, but the digital prototypes demonstrate how those concepts translate into usable interfaces. The dual-mode approach (Audio Tour vs Visual Tour) emerged from really listening to user needs rather than forcing a one-size-fits-all solution.

We are also proud of our research methodology. The combination of stakeholder interviews, accessibility research, and iterative prototyping gave us confidence that our solution addresses real needs. The comprehensive documentation of our process, from initial interviews to final prototypes, demonstrates thoughtful design thinking.

Areas Where We Could Have Done Better

Limited User Testing

While we conducted thorough stakeholder interviews with the volunteers, we did not have the opportunity to test our prototypes with actual blind or deaf users. This means our solution, while informed by research and best practices, has not been validated by the people who would actually use it.

What we would do differently: Reach out to local accessibility organizations earlier in the process to arrange user testing sessions with our target users.

Technical Implementation Gap

Our prototypes are beautifully designed but remain mockups. We have not built a working version that demonstrates technical feasibility, particularly around content management for volunteers or the QR code system integration.

What we would do differently: Develop at least a basic working prototype to validate technical assumptions and demonstrate implementation feasibility.

Overall Assessment

This project represents a solid foundation for inclusive design at Glasgow Botanic Gardens. Our solution is conceptually sound, grounded in real stakeholder needs, and designed with genuine care for accessibility. The dual-mode approach demonstrates sophisticated thinking about different user needs rather than superficial accommodation.

However, we recognize that good design does not end with prototypes. The true test of our solution would be in implementation, user adoption, and long-term sustainability. While we are confident in our research and design process, we remain humble about the gap between designed solutions and lived experience.

What we are most proud of: We approached this challenge with empathy, rigor, and genuine commitment to inclusion. Even if our solution is not perfect, it represents thoughtful design thinking applied to a meaningful problem.
