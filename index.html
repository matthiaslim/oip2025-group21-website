Gardens for
Everyone

A design project on making Glasgow Botanic Gardens accessible for blind and deaf visitors.

See Our Proposed Solution View Our Work

The Problem Statement

The Challenge: Volunteer guides at Glasgow Botanic Gardens want to enhance their tours to be more meaningful and enjoyable for deaf and/or blind visitors. However, existing tour formats rely heavily on visual and auditory elements, creating significant barriers for visitors with sensory impairments.

Key Barriers Identified:

For Blind Visitors: Lack of tactile elements, audio descriptions, and spatial orientation support
For Deaf Visitors: No sign language interpretation, visual information gaps, limited text-based content
Guide Challenges: Limited training in accessibility practices and lack of inclusive tour materials
Resource Constraints: Public venue with limited budget for accessibility improvements

How might we...

Design an accessible, cost-effective digital solution that empowers volunteer guides to deliver meaningful, inclusive botanical garden experiences for blind and deaf visitors, while enhancing rather than replacing human interaction?

Process Deep Dive

Discovery Challenges & Breakthroughs

Initial assumption: "We need to design one accessible tour." Reality from stakeholder interviews: Different disabilities require fundamentally different approaches.

Breakthrough moment: When volunteers explained how deaf and blind visitors need opposite information (visual detail vs. spatial audio), we realized one-size-fits-all was impossible.

Define Phase Iterations

Our "How Might We" statement evolved three times: from "create accessible tours" to "enhance existing tours" to "empower volunteers to deliver inclusive experiences."

Key shift: Recognizing volunteers as users, not just stakeholders, completely changed our solution approach.

Development Pivot Points

Original concept: Two separate systems—QR-guided tours for deaf visitors and physical audio devices for blind visitors. Pivot: Unified web-based platform with dual accessibility modes. This shift was driven by stakeholder feedback about maintenance complexity, cost constraints, and volunteer management realities.

Learning: Sometimes the most logical solution isn't the most practical one. Real-world constraints aren't obstacles—they're design criteria that lead to more sustainable solutions.

Delivery & Future Validation

Our prototypes demonstrate concept viability but represent beginning, not end, of design process. Implementation would require continuous user feedback and iterative refinement.

Next steps: Partner with disability organizations for user co-design sessions and technical validation through working prototypes.

Our Proposed Solution

Based on our process and research, here is our accessibility solution for Glasgow Botanic Gardens.

Visual Tour Mode (For Deaf Visitors)

A mobile-responsive web application featuring QR code scanning at key garden locations, providing access to British Sign Language (BSL) video interpretations, visual descriptions, high-resolution botanical photography, and text-based information tailored for deaf and hard-of-hearing visitors.

Audio Tour Mode (For Blind Visitors)

An accessible audio-first interface with location-based navigation menus, professional narration with rich botanical descriptions, ambient garden sounds, and screen reader-optimized controls. Users can select specific garden areas and plants for detailed auditory experiences.

Why This Solution Works

Our dual-mode approach directly addresses the different needs we discovered through our research. It is low-cost (leveraging visitors' smartphones), sustainable (easy for volunteers to manage), and truly inclusive (tailored experiences rather than one-size-fits-all).

From Process to Reflection

Our journey through research, design, and development has given us valuable insights about inclusive design, stakeholder collaboration, and the iterative nature of solving complex problems. Here are our honest reflections on what we learned, what we are proud of, and what we would do differently.

Reflections

Reflecting on our design journey, we share our proudest achievements, key learnings, and insights from working with our partners.

Reflections on Partner Interactions

Our collaboration with the Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens fundamentally changed how we approach design challenges. What started as a confident stride into "solving accessibility" became a masterclass in humility, collaboration, and the complex realities of inclusive design.

Key Themes That Emerged

1. The Gap Between Intention and Implementation

Every volunteer we spoke with expressed genuine desire to serve all visitors, but lacked practical tools and training to make this happen. We witnessed the frustration of caring people constrained by systems that weren't designed with accessibility in mind.

2. Resource Constraints as Design Drivers

The financial and operational limitations weren't just obstacles, but became creative catalysts. Learning that elaborate technology solutions were non-starters forced us to focus on elegantly simple approaches that could realistically be implemented and maintained.

3. Accessibility as Co-Design Process

Our biggest revelation was that accessibility isn't something you design for people, but something you design with them. The absence of actual disabled users in our process became increasingly uncomfortable as the project progressed.

4. Technology as Enabler, Not Solution

Initially, we were enticed by the possibility of high-tech solutions. Our partners helped us understand that the best technology becomes invisible, in a sense that it enables human connection and experience rather than dominating it.

Multiple Perspectives and Wider Context

Stakeholder Ecosystem Understanding

Our partner engagements unveiled a multifaceted array of considerations we had not foreseen. The volunteer guides were not merely tour providers; they were enthusiastic botanists, community champions, and supporters for accessibility, hindered by institutional constraints.

Wider Societal and Industry Context

Our research and discussions with partners illuminated how our project aligns with bigger initiatives aimed at inclusive design and universal access. The obstacles faced by the gardens are not distinctive; they instead illustrate systemic problems in the initial consideration of accessibility in public spaces, rather than treating it as an afterthought.

Policy Context: Recent UK accessibility legislation creating pressure and opportunities for cultural institutions to improve inclusion.

Technology Context: Smartphone ubiquity creating new possibilities for accessible experiences without specialized hardware.

Cultural Context: Growing awareness that accessibility benefits everyone, not just people with disabilities.

Genuine Learning Translated to Action

Fundamental Mindset Shifts
1
From "Solving For" to "Designing With"

Before: We approached the project as experts coming to solve a problem for people we'd never met.

After: We understood that meaningful accessibility requires ongoing collaboration with the communities we're designing for.

Actionable insight: Future projects must include budget and timeline for extensive user co-design sessions from day one.

2
From Feature Lists to Fundamental Experiences

Before: We focused on what features to include (QR codes, audio, video).

After: We learned to ask what experiences we were trying to create and work backward to appropriate tools.

Actionable insight: Start with emotional journey mapping before technical solution exploration.

3
From Perfect Solutions to Iterative Improvements

Before: We wanted to design the definitive accessible tour experience.

After: We realized that accessibility is an ongoing conversation, not a problem to be solved once.

Actionable insight: Design for continuous feedback and improvement rather than one-time deployment.

How We Changed as People and Collaborators

Working with the volunteers fundamentally changed our team dynamics. Initially, we were six individuals trying to avoid stepping on each other's toes. By the end, we were a functioning unit that could challenge each other's ideas constructively while building on collective insights.

The volunteers' expertise forced us to become better listeners. When Chrissy explained the practical realities of managing volunteer schedules, or when guides described the unique sensory qualities of different garden areas, we learned to absorb information that contradicted our assumptions without becoming defensive.

Most importantly, our partners taught us that successful design partnerships require vulnerability from all parties. They shared their uncertainties about accessibility; we shared our inexperience with real-world implementation. In that mutual honesty, something genuinely collaborative emerged.

The lasting impact: We now approach every design challenge by first asking, "Who are we not talking to that we should be?" The best solutions emerge from conversations we're initially uncomfortable having, with people whose expertise challenges our assumptions.

Reflecting on Our Work

What We're Genuinely Proud Of

We Actually Changed Direction When We Needed To

Our initial approach was to create two separate tour experiences: a QR-guided visual tour for deaf visitors and physical audio devices for blind visitors. We thought this made sense as different disabilities would probably need different solutions. But after interviewing Chrissy and the volunteers, we realized this approach would be a maintenance nightmare and too expensive to implement.

The volunteers explained they'd need to manage two completely different systems, keep audio devices charged and sanitized, and handle twice as much content creation. Instead of defending our "logical" approach, we stepped back and asked: "How can we create different experiences using the same underlying system?"

Why this matters: It's easy to fall in love with ideas that seem logical in theory. Learning to pivot when stakeholders explain real-world constraints—that's the difference between student projects and solutions that actually work.

We Learned to Disagree Constructively

Initially, some of us favored the physical audio device approach while others pushed for the QR system. We had different ideas about what would work best for users, and different concerns about technical feasibility and cost.

The turning point: Instead of each defending our preferred approach, we started asking "What are the actual constraints we need to work within?" This led us to find a solution that combined the best aspects of both ideas in a unified system.

We Admitted Our Limitations

Near the end, we realized we'd designed for blind and deaf users without ever testing with actual blind or deaf people. The ethical implications of this really bothered us.

What we did: Instead of pretending this wasn't a problem, we explicitly acknowledged it and explained how real implementation would require extensive user co-design from the start.

Our Solution Reflects Real Constraints

Our final QR-based approach isn't the most exciting technology, but it's what actually makes sense for this situation. Volunteers can manage it without technical training, visitors don't need to download anything, and it works on any smartphone.

We're proud that: We prioritized practical implementation over impressive features. The volunteers told us what they could realistically maintain, and we designed within those boundaries instead of ignoring them.

What We're Not Proud Of: The Uncomfortable Truths

The Missing Voices

Our biggest failure wasn't technical, it was human. We designed an accessibility solution without involving the people who would actually use it. Every decision about what blind users need came from research papers and assumptions. Every choice about deaf user experience came from design principles rather than lived experience.

This haunts us because we know better. The disability rights movement's foundational principle is "Nothing about us, without us," and we violated that completely. We told ourselves the 48-hour constraint justified this omission, but that's exactly the kind of thinking that perpetuates inaccessible design.

The hard truth: Our solution might look inclusive, but it's built on assumptions that could be completely wrong. Real accessibility requires disabled people at the design table from day one, not just as an afterthought for user testing.

The Prototype vs. Reality Gap

We fell in love with making our prototypes look polished and professional. The visual design is clean, the interactions are smooth, and the user flows seem logical. But they're just mockups. We never proved that QR codes can be reliably scanned in outdoor lighting conditions, or that our screen reader optimizations actually work across different devices and assistive technologies.

This is a classic student project trap (prioritizing presentation over validation). Our beautiful Figma files might impress in a portfolio, but they don't tell us whether the solution actually works for real people in real conditions.

What we should have done: Built a rough but functional prototype first, tested it in actual garden conditions, then refined based on real performance rather than aesthetic preferences.

The Narrow Definition of Accessibility

While we focused intensively on visual and auditory accessibility, we barely considered other important factors: mobility limitations that affect QR code scanning, cognitive differences that impact interface navigation, or temporary impairments like a broken arm or eye strain. Accessibility is intersectional, but our solution treats it as binary categories.

We also didn't consider cultural and linguistic accessibility. Our solution assumes English literacy, familiarity with smartphone interfaces, and comfort with technology—assumptions that exclude many potential garden visitors.

The learning: True accessibility means thinking about overlapping needs and multiple barriers simultaneously. Our "accessible" solution might create new barriers for people we never considered.

What This Project Really Taught Us

This project fundamentally changed how we understand the designer's role. We started thinking we were problem-solvers who would create elegant solutions for grateful users. We ended understanding that we're facilitators who help different types of expertise come together to address complex challenges.

Our solution has significant limitations, but it represents something we're genuinely proud of: a commitment to constraints over cleverness, collaboration over individual brilliance, and practical impact over theoretical elegance. We learned that the best design decisions often feel boring in the moment but prove their worth in the long term.

Moving forward: We'll never approach another design challenge without first asking who we're not including in our process. We'll never present a solution without acknowledging its limitations. And we'll never confuse making something look good with making something work well for real people in real situations.
