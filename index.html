Gardens for
Everyone

A design project on making Glasgow Botanic Gardens accessible for blind and deaf visitors.

See Our Proposed Solution View Our Work

The Problem Statement

The volunteer guides at Glasgow Botanic Gardens want to enhance their tours to be more meaningful and enjoyable for deaf and/or blind visitors. The core challenge is to understand the unique accessibility needs of these groups and adapt existing tour materials to better serve them, ensuring an inclusive experience for all.

Our User-Centric Framing

How might we help volunteer guides at the Glasgow Botanic Gardens offer meaningful and accessible tour experiences for blind and/or deaf visitors within the constraints of a public, low-cost environment?

Our Design Process

We adopted the Double Diamond model to guide our design journey.

1

Discover

We conducted an informal, face-to-face interview with our partner and researched existing solutions in other public attractions.

2

Define

We synthesized our research into key insights and formulated our "How Might We" statement to focus our efforts.

3

Develop

We brainstormed diverse ideas and created low-fidelity prototypes (sketches) to explore potential solutions.

4

Deliver

This final stage involves refining our proposed solution based on feedback and developing a clear implementation plan.

Our Proposed Solution

A low-cost, multi-faceted approach to enhance the tour experience.

For Deaf Visitors

Placing QR codes at key points of interest that link to a webpage with visual descriptions and British Sign Language (BSL) video interpretations.

For Blind Visitors

Developing a descriptive audio guide, accessible via their own devices, that includes rich descriptions, ambient sounds, and music to create a sensory experience.

Justification

This solution is suitable because it directly addresses the project's constraints. It is low-cost, as it leverages visitors' own smartphones and avoids expensive hardware. It is sustainable for volunteers, as the digital content can be easily managed and updated. Most importantly, it is inclusive by providing distinct, tailored experiences rather than a one-size-fits-all approach.

Reflections on Our Journey

Key learnings that have shaped our project.

🤝

On Partner Interactions

Our initial interview was a crucial learning moment. By using open-ended questions as taught in lectures ('The Mom Test' principles), we avoided leading the witness and gained genuine insights into the constraints and priorities of the volunteer guides. This taught us that understanding the needs of the *facilitators* is just as important as understanding the end-users.

🔧

On Prototyping

The feedback on our first pitch highlighted that our initial prototypes were more for demonstration than for learning. This was a key reflection. Our actionable insight is to now use low-fidelity prototyping as a tool to ask questions and test specific assumptions quickly, rather than just presenting a finished concept. We will explore more variations in Week 2 to fully leverage this method.

Our Story: What We're Proud Of

Reflecting on our work so far, we are most proud of our commitment to understanding the problem before jumping to solutions. It would have been simpler to propose a single piece of technology, but our initial research and partner interview quickly revealed the complexity of the challenge: designing for two distinct communities—deaf and blind visitors—with vastly different needs, all while respecting the constraints of a volunteer-run organization.

Embracing this complexity has been our biggest challenge, but also our greatest source of learning. It has forced us to think critically about what "accessibility" truly means. It's not a one-size-fits-all feature, but a nuanced approach that considers multiple perspectives. The process of defining our "How Might We" statement was a pivotal moment, focusing our efforts on a human-centered goal.

While our final solution is still developing, we are proud of the methodical foundation we have built. The journey ahead involves translating these early insights into actionable designs, and we are confident that by continuing to listen, iterate, and reflect, we can create an outcome that is genuinely helpful.
(This reflection will be expanded upon project completion).
